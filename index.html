A Decade of Heat:
California's Warming Years

How ten years of climate data reveal a state under pressure — and why it matters for every Californian.

Gabriel Borjas-Perez · Jared Parra · Dylan Craver · Christian Ramirez — UCSD

California has always lived with extremes. But the data now show something new: a steady, measurable shift toward a hotter baseline — with consequences already unfolding in its fires, droughts, and coastlines.

Using the CESM2 climate model under the high-emissions SSP5-8.5 scenario, we mapped yearly surface temperatures across California from 2015 through 2025. The visualization below compares each year against the 2015 baseline. Red signals warming, blue signals cooling. What emerges is not random variation — it is a trend.

Why It Matters

Three Ways Warming Changes Life in California

Temperature is not just a number. For California's 39 million residents, every fraction of a degree carries real consequences across water, fire, and human health.

🔥
Wildfire Risk
Hotter temperatures dry out vegetation, extending the fire season from a few months to nearly year-round. The eight largest wildfires in California history have all occurred since 2017. A single degree of additional warming can dramatically increase the area burned.
💧
Water Security
California relies on Sierra Nevada snowpack as a natural reservoir. As temperatures rise, more precipitation falls as rain and runs off immediately rather than storing as snow. This threatens the water supply for farms feeding a third of the nation and cities across the state.
🏥
Public Health
Heat waves kill more Californians than any other weather event. Extreme heat disproportionately affects elderly residents, outdoor workers, and low-income communities without air conditioning. Longer, hotter summers also worsen air quality through increased wildfire smoke and ozone formation.

Project Notes

About This Visualization

We built this visualization using 2015 as a baseline year, showing how temperatures across California have changed through 2025. Each grid point represents a CESM2 model output location; anomalies are computed by subtracting each location's 2015 temperature from the selected year's temperature at the same coordinate.

The visualization focuses on California specifically because it offers one of the most compelling and consequential regional stories in the dataset — a state large enough to contain deserts, mountains, farmland, and 39 million people, all responding differently to the same warming signal.

Future work may extend the timeline beyond 2025 using model projections, and explore higher-resolution regional breakdowns within California to show how communities in the Central Valley, coastal cities, and mountain counties face different warming futures.
